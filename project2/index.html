Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

John Collins — johnwcollins@ucsd.edu

Proposition: The NYPD complaint-review process is biased against Black civilians compared with White civilians.

FOR the Proposition

Key design decisions & rationale (–2 … +2):

  • Red fill between lines (Score –1) — visually amplifies every gap and makes it catch the eye.
  • Loaded title “Disparity in Substantiation” (–1) — frames difference as injustice before viewer inspects values.
  • Grid + full 0–100 % y-axis (+1.5) — keeps underlying numbers transparent, avoiding outright distortion.

AGAINST the Proposition

Key design decisions & rationale:

  • Placed counts panel first (Score –1) — frames Black civilians as filing far more complaints, suggesting equal substantiation rates are "fair."
  • Used dark black for Black complaint volume bar (Score –0.5) — adds visual weight and dominance to the disparity in complaint numbers.
  • Made substantiation-rate bars identical in color (Score –0.5) — subtly implies rates are effectively the same even though they're slightly different.
  • Compressed lower panel vertically (Score –1) — minimizes visual perception of the 3.4% gap, making both groups look nearly identical.

Final Reflection

Building two opposite stories from the same data felt like arguing both sides of a case. For the bias side, I picked the metric substantiation rate, used red shading, and wrote a strong title. These choices made a three-point gap seem more serious without touching the axis. The hardest part was realizing how little change was needed to shift perception. I had a tendency to go overboard with the visualization, but less is more.

Flipping the story back was simple: re-order the panels and zoom out. Showing complaint counts first made the workload difference obvious, and using a full 0–100 % axis on substantiation rates made the gap look small. What surprised me is how much order and layout can influence the viewer's attention without even changing the numbers.

I define an ethical visualization as one where a careful reader can still find the real pattern and numbers. Using color, order, or framing is persuasive but fair. Cutting axes, hiding groups, or flipping effects crosses the line into misleading. I think the key checks are showing the real values, labeling any filters, and making sure someone else could reproduce the chart.
